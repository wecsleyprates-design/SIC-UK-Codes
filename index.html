01. Problem Statement

Purpose

To explicitly define the misalignment between Worth's US-centric classification standard and the local infrastructure requirements of the UK market.

Worth currently classifies businesses using the North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) — a US-centric standard. For UK businesses, the only authoritative standard is the UK SIC (Standard Industrial Classification) 2007.

"The core problem: UK SIC data flows into our pipeline from vendors like OpenCorporates but is silently discarded by hardcoded NAICS filters. We are functionally 'Industry Blind' for 6 out of 10 UK businesses."

This document explains why this happens and provides the three-phase roadmap to fix it while establishing a model for every country in Worth's future expansion.

03. The Narrative Hub: Data Loss Story

Purpose

To trace the technical journey of a business record through the Worth pipeline and identify the exact moments where jurisdiction-specific data is dropped.

1

The Ingestion Point

A business record from OpenCorporates arrives in our service. The payload is rich: gb_sic-62020|us_naics-541511. The ground truth for the UK is physically inside our infrastructure.

2

The Logic Filter (The Bottleneck)

The code in integration-service begins to "read" the data. It is hardcoded to find the string "us_naics". When it sees "gb_sic", it does not recognize the prefix. It ignores it, drops the data from memory, and proceeds with the US code only.

3

The Pollution (Deceptive Vendors)

A secondary vendor like Trulioo checks the same business. It returns a 4-digit code: 7372. Because our system lacks a "Native UK Validator", it accepts this as a valid SIC—but 7372 is a US SIC 1972 code. We have now polluted a UK record with 50-year-old US data.

05. Fact Engine Logic & "Resolvency"

Purpose

To explain how Worth makes decisions when multiple vendors provide conflicting information, and define the terminology of fact arbitration.

Definition: What does "Resolve" mean?

In the FactEngine, to resolve a fact is the act of acting as a "Technical Jury." When seven vendors provide seven different answers, the engine must pick the single ground truth that will be stored in the database. It does this by weighing "Weight" against "Confidence."

Weight (Static)

A global trust multiplier for a vendor. Registry data (OpenCorporates) is given a 0.9 weight because it is official government data.

Confidence (Dynamic)

A runtime score (0.0 to 1.0) of how well the vendor's data matches the specific business being scored.

07. The 5-Experiment Victory Point

Purpose

To provide empirical scientific proof for the proposed solution, following the Layer 6 experimental roadmap from the workflow analysis.

EXP 1: REGISTRY BASELINE

Goal: Measure the baseline availability of UK SIC 2007 codes in the full 16M+ record OpenCorporates dataset.

SELECT COUNT(*) AS total, ROUND(100.0 * SUM(...) / COUNT(*), 2) AS coverage
FROM open_corporate.companies WHERE industry_uids LIKE '%gb_sic-%';
-- RESULT: 11,079,157 Records (66.49% Coverage)

Interpretation: Registry data is strong but not universal. 33.5% of the UK registry is missing SIC data at the source.

EXP 2: VENDOR FAILURE VETTING

Goal: Determine if Trulioo (our largest secondary source) returns UK SIC or US SIC pollution.

Action: Inspected 50 UK records in datascience.global_trulioo_uk_kyb.

Result: Found 4-digit codes labeled "US Standard Industry Code 1972". Verdict: Trulioo is REJECTED as a UK SIC source.

EXP 3: MANAGED PORTFOLIO GAP

Goal: Measure data coverage specifically for the curated businesses Worth actually scores.

Result: Found only 37.5% coverage. The 62.5% Gap was identified as the primary roadblock to business success in the UK.

EXP 4: FORMAT INTEGRITY CHECK

Goal: Confirm that the extracted codes match the modern SIC 2007 standard, not legacy systems.

SELECT DISTINCT SUBSTRING(industry_code_uids, ..., 5) AS code, COUNT(*) 
FROM companies GROUP BY 1 ORDER BY 2 DESC;
-- VERIFIED: 8299| (Support), 70229 (Consultancy) are true 2007 codes.
EXP 5: AI BRAINS VALIDATION

Goal: Prove that AI can "fill the gap" where registries are missing or stale.

Result: AI correctly identified 96090 (2007 standard) for businesses where the registry was still stuck with 8514 (1992 standard). AI is a Cleansing Tool.

10. Strategic Senior Answers

Purpose

To provide direct, high-level answers to the strategic questions required for global project sign-off.

Q1: Exactly what happens in the flow?

Answer: Data converges from 7 vendors. We filter for native prefixes, resolve conflicts based on Registry Weight (0.9) vs AI Weight (0.1), and save results to Case Service. The AI act as the "Gap Closer" for the 62.5% missing records.

Q2: How we handle New SIC UK?

Answer: By inserting a Parallel Gateway. Instead of forcing UK data through a NAICS-only pipe, we build a dedicated uk_sic_code pathway that mirrors NAICS but enforces 5-digit integrity.

Q3: A model for other countries?

Answer: This is the Worth Global Adapter. By replacing the 'gb_sic' prefix with 'de_wz' (Germany) or 'au_anzsic' (Australia), the entire pipeline instantly scales to the next jurisdiction.
